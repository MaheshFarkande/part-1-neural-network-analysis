## Task 6: Final Reflection
 # What role do weights and biases play in the model?
   Weights ($W$) function as scaling coefficients that determine the relational importance and impact of a specific input feature onto a hidden neuron. Biases ($b$) act as an offset value added to the linear sum ($z = Wx + b$), allowing the activation curve to shift left or right. Together, they form the parameters adjusted by backpropagation to fit data boundaries.
   # Why is an activation function required?
   Without non-linear activation functions (like $\text{ReLU}$ or $\text{Tanh}$), any stack of dense layers will mathematically reduce back into a single linear equation:$$y = W_2(W_1x + b_1) + b_2 = W_{new}x + b_{new}$$Non-linearity allows neural networks to approximate complex, non-linear boundaries required for real-world predictions.
   # What happens when the learning rate is too high or too low?
   Too High ($\alpha \ge 0.01$): Gradient updates take steps that are too large, causing the model to overshoot the optimal loss minimum. This leads to erratic training behavior, loss oscillation, or weight divergence ($\text{NaN}$ loss values).Too Low ($\alpha \le 0.0001$): Updates are infinitely small, resulting in slow training speeds. The model risks timing out or getting trapped permanently inside shallow local minima or saddle points.
   # Did your model show signs of underfitting or overfitting? Explain.
   Our baseline model and Experiment 3 suffered from underfitting regarding the minority class, completely failing to generalize any characteristics about churned customers due to class imbalance.Conversely, Experiment 1 and Experiment 2 showed explicit signs of overfitting. Both achieved $100.00\%$ accuracy on training inputs while their validation losses surged dramatically higher than their training losses. The extra parameters in Experiment 1 memorized the sample patterns instead of identifying broad rules, limiting its real-world generalization to a minor fraction of the churned demographic.
